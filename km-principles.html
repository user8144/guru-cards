KM Principles
12 principles that form the foundation for an agile, innovative org — fostering a culture of learning and unlocking the full potential of employees in the Knowledge Age.
1
Choice
▼
Giving individuals the freedom to choose their tasks increases motivation and fosters innovation. Passion-driven work leads to better engagement, creativity, and outcomes.
2
Throw Away the Box
▼
Focus on the ultimate objective, not a rigid framework. Instead of "get wheels," say "land safely." Broader goals unlock more creative, effective solutions.
3
Supply and Demand
▼
KM fails when orgs assume they know what people need. Align resources with actual employee needs — user-driven, not top-down.
4
The Crapper Principle
▼
Don't build repositories for the sake of it. Every piece of knowledge captured should serve a clear purpose and be actionable. Avoid knowledge graveyards.
5
Teams and Teamwork
▼
Strong, well-organized teams are central to the Knowledge Age. They pool knowledge, foster innovation, and enable rapid adaptation to change.
6
Specialization
▼
As tasks grow more complex, deep expertise beats generalism. Focused specialization leads to higher-quality outcomes and greater efficiency.
7
Personal Knowledge Management (PKM)
▼
Org success depends on individuals managing their own knowledge. PKM encourages ownership of personal development — staying engaged and contributing fully.
8
Knowledge Is Messy and Self-Organizing
▼
You can't isolate knowledge neatly. But given the right environment, it organizes naturally around a group's identity and purpose. Remove the barriers and let it structure itself.
9
Knowledge Seeks Community
▼
Knowledge happens best in community and travels via shared language. Build environments where people connect, and invest in the language that makes knowledge flow.
10
Looser Is Better
▼
The tighter you control knowledge, the more it slips away. Adaptable systems survive longer. Capture what matters — but don't over-engineer. Rigid control wastes significant resources.
11
There Is No One Solution
▼
No silver bullet. Knowledge is always changing. The best approach keeps things moving while keeping options open. Start with the right definition and support it at multiple levels.
12
Knowledge Has a Lifecycle
▼
Knowledge doesn't grow forever — it becomes irrelevant, dies, or gets lost. Unlearning and retiring outdated knowledge is just as important as capturing new knowledge.
